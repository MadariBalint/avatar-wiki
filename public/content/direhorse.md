The **Direhorse** (Na’vi name: **Pa’li**; pronunciation: [ˈpaʔ.li]) is a Pandoran nectarivore, vaguely horse-like in appearance, with a very long neck and small head.
The [[Na'vi]] are excellent riders of the direhorse, and it, with its six legs, is a swift and nimble mount that is well-adapted to the rugged Pandoran terrain.

## Anatomy

The direhorse is a third larger than even the largest Terran draft horses such as the Shire, the Clydesdale, or the Percheron, which makes it substantially larger than the largest horse ever recorded on Earth and approximately the same size as an Asian elephant.

The direhorse, as with most complex fauna on Pandora, has two queues that emerge from either side of the skull. These have feathery tips that are covered in tiny cilia, which undulate constantly in search of the queues of other direhorses as they move nearby. Xenobiologists believe that the touch of queues is not only for pleasure and affection between individuals but is also a means of transferring information about food sources and potential dangers. Herds often move in unison shortly after touching queues.

Like many Pandoran animals, the direhorse has sets of operculi running down its neck. These are used for breathing, instead of a trachea like many Terran animals. The direhorse also has a sail-like "mane" that may play a part in signaling and mating rituals.

The direhorse has four eyes; the smaller two have rectangular pupils, much like Terran horses.

In _James Cameron’s Avatar: The Game_, the direhorse is shown to be sexually dimorphic. Males are depicted as having red and teal colored skin, while females are depicted as having blue and yellow. However, the game is now considered non-canon.

## Behavior

Direhorses are easily startled. When all six legs work in unison, they can reach speeds of up to 95 kilometres per hour (or 59 miles per hour).

When wild, direhorses move together in a loose herd through forests and grasslands, feeding on tree bark, shrubs, and nectar from flowers. Herds numbering in the dozens have been spotted from aircraft, but evidence (including scatological and plant impact) suggests that herds of more than one hundred are not uncommon.

Direhorses have fast reaction times, can turn quickly, and can leap large distances. This makes them ideal mounts for the Na’vi.

## Feeding

Unlike horses on Earth, the direhorse is a nectarivore that uses its long snout, as well as its long giraffe-like tongue, to feed on nectar from plants such as the direhorse pitcher plant, with which it has a symbiotic relationship. The direhorse drinks the nectar from the plant and pollinates it in return. As the plant is carnivorous, the direhorse may also acquire some protein from insects it has trapped.
